Experimental Evaluation of Security Attacks on Self-Driving Car Platforms
A Research Study Using NVIDIA Jetson & Yahboom Autonomous Vehicles
Abstract: Autonomous vehicle systems present critical attack surfaces at both the perception and communication layers. This study moves beyond theoretical simulation to provide a comprehensive experimental evaluation of on-device attacks, quantifying their distinct operational impacts on real-world driving platforms. We implement a suite of adversarial (FGSM, PGD), Man-in-the-Middle (MitM), and Denial-of-Service (DoS) attacks on a ResNet-18 based control system deployed on JetRacer and Yahboom vehicles. Using a set of specialized metrics, we establish a novel framework for identifying the unique "fingerprint" of each attack class. Our results reveal that attacks manifest in dramatically different ways. We found that Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) attacks pose a dual threat, causing severe steering deviation (0.42) while also acting as a resource-exhaustion attack that increases processing time by over 3,300%. In contrast, the most effective attack for pure control hijacking was a MitM assault on the model's output, which achieved the highest steering deviation (0.76) with negligible performance cost. DoS attacks showed a third distinct fingerprint, having minimal impact on steering but crippling the vehicle's responsiveness with a nearly 90% drop in frames per second (FPS). These findings demonstrate that a one-size-fits-all security approach is insufficient. The study highlights the urgent need for multi-modal defense systems capable of detecting anomalies across vehicle behavior, system performance, and network traffic to secure autonomous platforms against diverse threat vectors.
